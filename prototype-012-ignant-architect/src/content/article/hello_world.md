## The Site

Naoshima's southeastern shore offered a narrow strip of reclaimed land between a granite hillside and the tidal flats of the Seto Inland Sea. The museum sits at this threshold — not on the water, but *of* it.

### Material Palette

The primary structure is board-formed concrete, cast against cedar planks salvaged from a demolished warehouse on the island. The formwork grain registers the timber's history into every wall surface, creating a texture that reads differently in morning light than at dusk.

### Spatial Sequence

Visitors enter through a compressed corridor — ceiling height barely 2.2 meters — before the main gallery opens upward to 9 meters, framing a panoramic view of the sea through a single, uninterrupted glass wall. The compression-release sequence is borrowed from traditional Japanese garden design, where narrow paths precede open vistas.

### Light

Clerestory windows along the north elevation wash the interior walls with diffused daylight. No artificial light is used in the permanent collection galleries during operating hours. The building's orientation was calibrated to avoid direct sun on any artwork surface throughout the year.
